What is Prehospital CTAS?
01

⚡ Five-Level Scale

CTAS is based on a five-level scale. Level 1 (Resuscitation) = sickest. Level 5 (Non-Urgent) = least ill.

02

📋 Chief Complaint Driven

CTAS level is determined by the patient's presenting complaint and its relationship to potential sentinel events.

03

🔧 Modifiers Refine Acuity

First and second order modifiers further refine acuity beyond the chief complaint to capture subtle clinical signs.

04

🕐 Applied Minimum Twice

Prehospital CTAS is applied and documented a minimum of 2 times per patient — on arrival and at departure/after intervention.

05

👤 Adult & Pediatric

Separate frameworks for adults and pediatric patients (<14 years) with age-specific vital sign norms and the Pediatric Assessment Triangle.

06

⚖️ Non-Judgmental Approach

Paramedics must remain consistent and non-judgmental. Prejudice can increase patient risk through incorrect acuity assignment.

The 4-Step CTAS Process
1

⚡ Quick Look

Immediate visual assessment on arrival. Can assign CTAS 1 only. Identifies patients requiring immediate resuscitation before any history is taken.

2

📋 Presenting Complaint

Establish the chief complaint and match it to the CTAS complaint list. This gives the baseline CTAS level before modifiers are applied.

3

🔧 First Order Modifiers

Apply vital signs, pain scale, LOC (GCS/AVPU), bleeding disorders, MOI, and temperature. These can upgrade or confirm the initial level.

4

🔬 Second Order Modifiers

Apply complaint-specific modifiers (blood glucose, hypertension, obstetrics, mental health). These cannot downgrade a level already set higher.

"Quick Look can only assign CTAS 1. For all other levels, the full 4-step process is mandatory — no shortcuts."

— CTAS Prehospital Protocol · Dr. Metwalley Amin Helwa

The Five CTAS Levels
1
🔴 Resuscitation

Threats to life or limb with imminent risk of deterioration requiring immediate aggressive interventions.

  • Arrested or pre/post-arrest patients
  • Active resuscitation required
  • Airway, ventilation, or circulatory support
  • ABCs requiring immediate intervention
⏱ Immediate — 0 min
2
🟠 Emergent

Potential threat to life, limb or function requiring rapid interventions and condition-specific controlled medical acts.

  • Serious illness/injury with potential deterioration
  • May require resuscitation
  • Controlled acts often applied in field
  • Rapid further intervention required
⏱ Very Urgent — 15 min
3
🟡 Urgent

Could potentially progress to serious problem requiring emergency interventions. May affect ability to function.

  • Normal vital signs
  • Suggests more serious acute process
  • Moderate acute pain (4–7/10)
  • Pain scales most commonly used here
⏱ Urgent — 30 min
4
🟢 Less-Urgent

Conditions relating to patient age, distress, or potential for deterioration that would benefit from intervention.

  • Stable vital signs, lower pain scales
  • Chief complaint not acutely serious
  • Acute intervention not urgent
  • Many chronic illness patients without exacerbation
⏱ Less Urgent — 60 min
5
🔵 Non-Urgent

Acute but non-urgent, or part of a chronic problem. Investigations or interventions could be delayed.

  • Truly minor complaints
  • No immediate risk to patient
  • Limited degree of pain
  • Medical directives use is limited
⏱ Non-Urgent — 120 min

⚖️
Rules for Assigning CTAS
1

📝 Rule #1 — Minimum Two Scores

  • CTAS #1: On arrival — before interventions. Marker for response times.
  • CTAS #2: At departure / after intervention — determines destination.
  • Both are mandatory on E-PCR.
  • Additional scores if condition changes en route.
2

📢 Rule #2 — Reported CTAS Level

Report the first CTAS level to the receiving ED, or the updated one if condition changed. Notify ED of any changes en route.

3

🔴 Rule #3 — VSA & Resuscitation

For a VSA patient who is resuscitated, CTAS must stay as CTAS 1 permanently. The initial severity must be recorded regardless of outcome.

4

☠️ Rule #4 — Obviously Dead

Patient determined "obviously dead" on arrival: CTAS must be assigned and documented as zero (0) on E-PCR.

5

🕐 Transport Time Considerations

  • Short transport (<10 min): May not have time to update CTAS before arrival.
  • Long transport (≥20 min): Greater likelihood of condition change — update ED of CTAS changes.
6

⚠️ Hard-to-Assign Patients

If patient doesn't fit any category: consult SRCA medical control or use clinical judgement. Never leave a patient unassigned.

🚑 Transport Destination Guidelines
1

Resuscitation: Nearest / closest most appropriate facility capable of required care. No exceptions unless directed by medical control.

2

Emergent: Nearest / closest most appropriate facility based on communication between paramedics, medical control, and receiving facility.

3·4·5

Urgent to Non-Urgent: Most available receiving facility. Greater flexibility based on availability and patient preference.

First & Second Order Modifiers
First Order Modifiers are applied once the presenting complaint is determined. They are divided into Group A (ABCs & Vital Signs) and Group B (Condition-Specific). Apply all that are relevant.
❤️

A — Heart Rate

  • CTAS 1 HR <40 or >180 bpm · Absent pulse
  • CTAS 2 HR 40–50 or 150–180 bpm
  • CTAS 3 HR 51–60 or 120–149 bpm
  • Pediatric: use age-specific HR norms
🩺

A — Blood Pressure (Systolic)

  • CTAS 1 SBP <70 mmHg (adult) · Absent
  • CTAS 2 SBP 70–90 mmHg · Haemodynamic compromise
  • CTAS 3 SBP 90–100 mmHg with symptoms
🫁

B — Breathing / Respiratory

  • CTAS 1 SpO₂ <90% · RR <10 or >35/min · Agonal breathing · Central cyanosis
  • CTAS 2 SpO₂ 90–94% · Moderate distress · Accessory muscle use · Marked tachypnoea
  • CTAS 3 SpO₂ 95–97% · Mild distress · Tachypnoea without severe distress
  • CTAS 4 SpO₂ ≥98% · No distress · Mild wheeze or cough only
🧠

D — Level of Consciousness (GCS)

  • CTAS 1 GCS ≤8 · Unresponsive (P or U on AVPU)
  • CTAS 2 GCS 9–13 · Responds to Voice (V on AVPU)
  • CTAS 3 GCS 14 · Confused/disoriented
  • Use AVPU: Alert · Voice · Pain · Unresponsive
🔥

Pain Scale (0–10)

  • CTAS 2 Severe pain: 8–10/10
  • CTAS 3 Moderate pain: 4–7/10 (most common modifier)
  • Use age-appropriate scales for pediatric patients
  • Document on E-PCR consistently
🩸

Bleeding Disorders

  • Applies to: haemophilia · anticoagulants · liver failure
  • Higher risk for serious bleeding complications
  • Upgrade CTAS level by one if bleeding disorder present
  • Document on E-PCR
🚗

Mechanism of Injury (MOI)

  • CTAS 2 High-energy: MVC >60 km/h · ejection · rollover · death in vehicle · pedestrian struck · fall >6m
  • CTAS 3 Moderate: MVC <60 km/h · fall 3–6m · assault with weapon
  • Predicts occult injury even with normal vitals
Second Order Modifiers are complaint-specific, applied after first order modifiers. They cannot downgrade a CTAS level already set higher.
🩸

Blood Glucose

  • CTAS 2 BG <60 mg/dl with altered LOC (hypoglycaemia)
  • CTAS 2 BG >324 mg/dl (severe hyperglycaemia)
  • CTAS 3 BG <60 without altered LOC
  • Always assess BG in altered LOC patients
📈

Hypertension

  • CTAS 2 SBP >220 or DBP >130 mmHg
  • CTAS 3 SBP 180–220 or DBP 110–130 mmHg
  • Hypertensive crisis with end-organ symptoms → CTAS 2
🤰

Obstetrics (≥20 Weeks)

  • Active labor · vaginal bleeding · severe abdominal pain → CTAS 2
  • Decreased fetal movement · mild contractions → CTAS 3
  • Supine hypotension: position on left side
🧠

Mental Health Modifiers

  • CTAS 2 Actively suicidal/homicidal with plan & means
  • CTAS 2 Severe agitation · violent · danger to self/others
  • CTAS 3 Suicidal ideation without plan · moderate agitation

👶
Pediatric CTAS (<14 Years)
Children are not small adults. Pediatric CTAS requires age-specific vital sign norms, the Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT), and age-appropriate pain scales.
🔺 Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)
👁️

Appearance

Tone · Interactiveness · Consolability · Look/Gaze · Speech/Cry. Abnormal appearance = significant illness until proven otherwise.

🫁

Work of Breathing

Abnormal sounds · Abnormal positioning · Retractions · Nasal flaring. Increased work = respiratory distress.

🩸

Circulation to Skin

Pallor · Mottling · Cyanosis. Abnormal skin circulation = shock or severe illness.

Understanding CTAS Through Analogies
✈️

The Airport Analogy

Just as airports prioritize flights by emergency status — not arrival time — CTAS prioritizes patients by clinical urgency. A CTAS 1 patient is the equivalent of a "Mayday" call: everything stops, all resources deploy immediately.

🏗️

The Building Fire Analogy

A fire chief doesn't treat all rooms equally — they assess which areas are most dangerous and allocate resources accordingly. CTAS gives the paramedic the same systematic framework to allocate urgency and resources.

🎼

The Orchestra Analogy

CTAS is the conductor's score. Each modifier is an instrument. The paramedic is the conductor — reading all signals simultaneously, synthesizing them into a single, decisive clinical judgment: the CTAS level.

⚖️

The Legal Analogy

Just as a judge weighs evidence before a verdict, the paramedic weighs clinical evidence (complaint + modifiers) before a CTAS verdict. Both require consistency, objectivity, and documentation.

"CTAS is not a form to fill — it is a clinical decision that protects the patient from the moment of first contact to the moment of ED handover."

When & When Not to Use CTAS

✅ CTAS IS Used When:

  • Any patient contact by SRCA paramedic or EMT
  • All emergency calls — medical, trauma, obstetric, pediatric
  • Inter-facility transfers
  • Walk-in patients at EMS stations
  • Mass casualty incidents (modified CTAS/START triage)
  • Any patient requiring E-PCR documentation

🚫 CTAS is NOT Used When:

  • Patient is obviously dead on arrival (CTAS 0 instead)
  • Patient refuses assessment and transport (document refusal)
  • Non-patient contacts (bystander assistance only)
  • Administrative or non-clinical calls

⚠️ CTAS is NOT a Refusal Tool

CTAS level does NOT determine whether a patient is transported. A CTAS 5 patient still has the right to transport. CTAS determines urgency and destination — not eligibility for care.

📞
Role of the Medical Director

🏥 Oversight & Quality Assurance

The SRCA Medical Director oversees the clinical quality of CTAS application across all paramedic teams. Regular audits of E-PCR data identify patterns of under-triage or over-triage.

📞 Medical Control Consultation

When a paramedic encounters a patient who is difficult to assign a CTAS level to, contacting SRCA medical control is the appropriate step. The medical director or designee provides guidance.

📋 Protocol Authority

The Medical Director has authority over all CTAS-related protocols, including destination decisions, controlled medical acts, and deviation from standard protocols in exceptional circumstances.

📚 Education & Training

Responsible for ensuring all SRCA paramedics and EMTs receive initial and ongoing CTAS training. Competency verification is a medical director responsibility.

Beyond CTAS: The Bigger Picture
🤝

ED Handover Communication

CTAS is the universal language between prehospital and in-hospital teams. Communicating the CTAS level on arrival gives the ED team an immediate, standardized clinical picture before the patient is even unloaded.

📊

System Performance Measurement

CTAS data drives EMS system quality improvement. Response time benchmarks, resource allocation decisions, and staffing models are all built on aggregated CTAS data from E-PCR records.

⚖️

Legal & Medico-Legal Protection

A correctly documented CTAS level is your clinical and legal record. In cases of adverse outcomes, the E-PCR with both CTAS scores demonstrates systematic, evidence-based clinical decision-making.

🌍

International Standardization

CTAS is used across Canada and adapted internationally. SRCA's adoption aligns Saudi prehospital EMS with global best practices, enabling benchmarking against international EMS systems.

📱

Digital Integration (e-PCR)

CTAS is fully integrated into the SRCA electronic Patient Care Report. Both mandatory scores, all modifiers applied, and the presenting complaint are captured digitally for audit and quality assurance.

Clinical Deceptions in CTAS
⚠️ Clinical deceptions are presentations that appear stable or benign but conceal life-threatening conditions. Recognizing these patterns is the mark of an experienced paramedic.

🎭 The "Looks Fine" Deception

A patient who appears well-dressed, calm, and communicative can still be CTAS 1 or 2. Appearance is not acuity. A compensating trauma patient may look fine until sudden cardiovascular collapse.

💊 The Normal Vitals Fallacy

Normal vital signs do NOT exclude serious illness. Young, fit patients can maintain normal vitals until 30–40% blood volume loss. High-energy MOI mandates CTAS 2 regardless of vital signs.

👶 The Quiet Child

A quiet, unresponsive child is not calm — it is critically ill. Children compensate brilliantly until sudden decompensation. Silence in a sick child is a red flag emergency sign.

🧓 The Elderly Patient Deception

Elderly patients on beta-blockers may not mount a tachycardia in shock. Baseline hypertension means a "normal" BP of 120/80 may represent severe hypotension for that patient. Always compare to their baseline.

🩺 The "Just Anxiety" Trap

Anxiety is a diagnosis of exclusion. Tachycardia, dyspnoea, and chest tightness must be fully evaluated before attributing to anxiety. PE, ACS, and pneumothorax all mimic anxiety presentations.

🔄 The Repeat Caller Bias

Familiarity breeds complacency. A patient you have seen 10 times for "the same complaint" can present their 11th call as a CTAS 1. Anchor bias kills. Begin every assessment fresh.

🔍
Common CTAS Pitfalls

1️⃣ Skipping the Full 4-Step Process

Going directly from Quick Look to a CTAS level without completing the complaint → first order → second order process leads to systematic under-triage.

2️⃣ Not Documenting Both CTAS Scores

Both CTAS #1 (on arrival) and CTAS #2 (at departure/after intervention) are mandatory on the E-PCR. Missing either score is a documentation failure and a protocol violation.

3️⃣ Allowing Prejudice to Influence CTAS

Age, gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, or past behaviour must never influence CTAS assignment. CTAS is an objective clinical tool — not a social judgment. Prejudice increases patient risk.

4️⃣ Using Quick Look for CTAS 2–5

Quick Look can only assign CTAS Level 1. For CTAS 2–5, the full 4-step process is mandatory — no shortcuts.

5️⃣ Incorrect Pediatric Vital Sign Interpretation

Using adult vital sign norms for children leads to under-triage. A heart rate of 130 is normal for a toddler but alarming in an adult. Always use age-specific pediatric norms for patients under 14.

6️⃣ Failing to Communicate CTAS to Receiving ED

The CTAS level must be communicated to the receiving institution — not just documented on the E-PCR. The ED needs this information to allocate resources before the patient arrives.

7️⃣ Ignoring Temperature as a Modifier

Temperature is a first order modifier. When a thermometer is available, temperature must be assessed — especially in pediatric patients and suspected sepsis.

8️⃣ Retroactively Changing CTAS #1

After a VSA patient is resuscitated, changing CTAS #1 to reflect the improved state is explicitly prohibited. Rule #3: VSA patients must remain CTAS 1 permanently.

💎
Pearls of Wisdom
🚨

CTAS 1 Waits for No One

When Quick Look reveals a CTAS 1 patient, every second of delay is a second of preventable harm. The 4-step process is bypassed — immediate intervention is the only correct response. Act first. Document after.

🎭

The Patient Who Looks Fine

A well-dressed, calm, communicative patient can still be CTAS 2. Appearance is not acuity. The compensating trauma patient looks fine until they don't. Trust the mechanism. Trust the modifiers. Never trust appearance alone.

📊

Normal Vitals Are Not Normal Enough

Normal vital signs do not exclude serious illness. Young, fit patients can maintain normal vitals until 30–40% blood volume loss. High-energy MOI mandates CTAS 2 regardless of vital signs.

👶

The Quiet Child Is Not a Calm Child

Children compensate brilliantly until they suddenly decompensate. A quiet, unresponsive child is critically ill. Silence in a sick child is a red flag, not reassurance.

⚖️

CTAS Is Not a Judgment — It Is a Measurement

CTAS must be applied with the same objectivity as a blood pressure reading. Age, appearance, behaviour, or social status must never influence the level. Prejudice is a clinical error.

🔄

CTAS Is Dynamic — Reassess Always

A CTAS 3 patient can become CTAS 1 in minutes. The second CTAS score exists for exactly this reason. Never assume the first score is the final score. Reassess. Reassess. Reassess.

📝

If It Isn't Documented, It Didn't Happen

The E-PCR is your clinical record, your legal protection, and the hospital's first window into your patient. Both CTAS scores are mandatory. Every modifier applied must be documented. Documentation is clinical care.

🔁

Every Patient Is a New Story

Familiarity is the enemy of accuracy. The patient you have seen 10 times before can present their 11th call as a CTAS 1. Anchor bias kills. Begin every assessment with fresh eyes.

📚

Document Everything — Always

The E-PCR is not bureaucracy — it is your clinical record and legal protection. Both CTAS scores are mandatory. If it isn't written, it didn't happen.

🌟

CTAS Is a Language — Speak It Fluently

When you say "CTAS 2" to the receiving ED, you communicate urgency, physiology, and resource need in a single number. Master CTAS and you master the language of prehospital urgency.

💜

Children Are Not Small Adults

Pediatric CTAS requires age-specific vital sign norms, the PAT, and age-appropriate pain scales. Apply pediatric thresholds — never adult norms — to children under 14.

🎯

MOI Is a Modifier — Not a Diagnosis

High-energy MOI mandates CTAS 2 even if the patient is walking and talking. The body compensates. Internal injuries are invisible. "Until proven otherwise" is the rule.

⚡

Speed and Accuracy Are Not Opposites

The 4-step CTAS process is designed to be rapid and systematic simultaneously. With practice, it becomes second nature. A well-trained paramedic assigns CTAS accurately in under 60 seconds.

"In prehospital medicine, the paramedic who masters CTAS does not merely classify patients — they protect them. Every correct CTAS level is an act of advocacy for the patient who cannot yet speak for themselves in the emergency department."
