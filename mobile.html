Canadian Triage and Acuity Scale — CTAS

هو أحد الأنظمة المعتمدة عالميًا لتصنيف المرضى وفقًا لدرجة احتياجهم للرعاية الطبية العاجلة. يتيح هذا النظام للأطباء والأخصائيين والمسعفين اتخاذ قرارات سريعة ومدروسة بشأن أولوية التدخل، بناءً على خطورة الحالة الصحية للمريض والوقت المناسب لرؤيته وتقديم العلاج.

درجات الفرز:

CTAS 1

إنعاش: تتطلب تدخلاً طبيًا فوريًا؛ مثل توقف القلب أو التنفس.

CTAS 2

حرجة: تحتاج لتدخل عاجل خلال دقائق؛ مثل ألم صدري حاد مع علامات خطورة.

CTAS 3

طارئة: تحتاج إلى تقييم سريع ولكنها مستقرة نسبيًا مثل: كسور أو آلام متوسطة الشدة.

CTAS 4

عاجلة: لا تهدد الحياة بشكل مباشر ويمكن الانتظار لفترة أطول مثل: جروح سطحية بسيطة.

CTAS 5

غير عاجلة: مشكلات بسيطة يمكن التعامل معها بعد فترة انتظار مثل: نزلات البرد أو آلام طفيفة.

What is Prehospital CTAS?

01

⚡ Five-Level Scale

CTAS is based on a five-level scale. Level 1 (Resuscitation) represents the sickest patients, while Level 5 (Non-Urgent) represents the least ill group.

02

📋 Chief Complaint Driven

The CTAS level is determined by establishing a relationship between the patient's presenting complaint and the potential causes defined by sentinel events.

03

🔧 Modifiers Refine Acuity

First and second order modifiers further refine the application of an acuity level, going beyond the chief complaint to capture subtle clinical signs.

04

🕑 Applied Minimum Twice

Unlike ED triage, prehospital CTAS is applied and documented a minimum of two (2) times per patient — on arrival and at departure/after intervention.

05

👤 Adult & Pediatric

Separate but parallel frameworks exist for adults and pediatric patients (<14 years), with age-specific vital sign norms and the Pediatric Assessment Triangle.

06

⚖ Non-Judgmental Approach

Attitude and empathy are critical. Paramedics must remain consistent and non-judgmental. Prejudice can increase patient risk through incorrect acuity assignment.

CTAS Assessment Process

1

👁 STEP 1 — Quick Look

The first step in the CTAS process. In some cases, the CTAS level can be determined quickly by a simple "Quick Look" when the presenting complaint is obvious.

  • Applies primarily to critically ill patients who appear in extremis on initial contact.
  • "Quick Look" can only be used to place a patient in CTAS Level 1.
  • Many patients who appear well may have subtle signs that modifiers will identify — placing them in a more acute level than Quick Look suggests.
◆ Is the patient obviously CTAS Level 1?
YES → Assign CTAS 1 immediately NO → Continue to Step 2
2

📋 STEP 2 — Presenting Complaint (CEDIS Category)

Determine the presenting complaint based on the CEDIS category. The presenting complaint should only be used to place the patient into CTAS Level 1 — as with the Quick Look.

  • Example: Cardiovascular patient with chest pain with cardiac features and signs of decompensation → CTAS 1.
  • For most patients, modifiers will be needed to accurately determine the final CTAS level.
3

🔧 STEP 3 — First Order Modifiers

Applied once the presenting complaint is determined. Applied in two groups:

Group A — ABCs & Vital Signs
  • A & B — Respiratory Distress (RR, SpO₂, effort)
  • C — Hemodynamic Stability (HR, BP, perfusion)
  • D — Level of Consciousness (GCS)
  • T — Temperature (if available)
Group B — Condition-Specific
  • Pain (scale 0–10)
  • Bleeding Disorders (hemophilia, anticoagulants, liver failure)
  • Mechanism of Injury (MOI)

⚠ Look for all modifiers that apply — do not stop at the first one found.

4

📈 STEP 4 — Second Order Modifiers

More complaint-specific modifiers applied after first order modifiers. Applied in two types:

  • Type 1 — Supplementary: Blood glucose (<60 or >324 mg/dl), Hypertension, Selected adult modifiers
  • Type 2 — Complaint-Specific: Obstetrics (≥20 weeks gestation), Mental health modifiers

⚠ A second order modifier must NOT downgrade the CTAS level when a higher level is already indicated by the presenting complaint or first order modifiers.

✓

🎯 ASSIGN FINAL CTAS LEVEL

Based on the Quick Look, presenting complaint, and all applicable modifiers, assign the final CTAS level (1–5). Document on the E-PCR. Notify the receiving ED of the level and any changes en route.

🏁

The Five CTAS Levels

1
Resuscitation

Conditions that are threats to life or limb or have imminent risk of deterioration requiring immediate aggressive interventions.

  • Arrested or pre/post-arrest patients
  • Requires active resuscitation
  • Airway support, ventilation, or circulatory support
  • Problems with ABCs requiring immediate intervention
⏳ Immediate — 0 min
2
Emergent

Conditions that are a potential threat to life, limb or function requiring rapid medical interventions and condition-specific controlled medical acts.

  • Serious illness or injury with potential for deterioration
  • May then require resuscitation
  • Controlled acts often applied in the field
  • Requires further rapid intervention
⏳ Very Urgent — 15 min
3
Urgent

Conditions that could potentially progress to a serious problem requiring emergency interventions. May affect ability to function.

  • Normal vital signs
  • Presenting problem suggests more serious acute process
  • Moderate acute pain (scale 4–7/10)
  • Pain scales most commonly used in this category
⏳ Urgent — 30 min
4
Less-Urgent

Conditions relating to patient age, distress, or potential for deterioration that would benefit from intervention or reassurance.

  • Stable vital signs and lower pain scales
  • Chief complaint not as acutely serious
  • Acute intervention not as urgent
  • Many chronic illness patients without significant exacerbation
⏳ Less Urgent — 60 min
5
Non-Urgent

Conditions that may be acute but non-urgent, or part of a chronic problem. Investigations or interventions could be delayed.

  • Truly minor complaints
  • No immediate risk to the patient
  • Limited degree of pain
  • Potential use of medical directives is limited
⏳ Non-Urgent — 120 min
⚖️

Rules for Assigning CTAS Levels

1

📝 Rule #1 — Minimum Two Scores

A minimum of two (2) CTAS scores will be applied to each patient:

  • First CTAS: On arrival — reflects initial condition before paramedic interventions. Serves as a marker for response times.
  • Second CTAS: At departure from scene or after intervention — aids in determining destination and reflects change in condition.
  • Both scores are mandatory and must be documented on the E-PCR.
  • Additional scores may be needed if condition changes en route.
2

📢 Rule #2 — Reported CTAS Level

The CTAS level reported to the receiving institution is the first CTAS level or the updated one if the patient's condition has changed.

Paramedics must notify the ED of any changes in CTAS level en route to allow the ED staff to prepare appropriate resources.

3

🔴 Rule #3 — VSA & Resuscitation

For a patient who is Vital Signs Absent (VSA) on arrival and who is resuscitated, the CTAS must stay as CTAS 1.

This rule ensures that the initial severity of the patient's condition is permanently recorded, regardless of the outcome of resuscitation efforts.

4

☠ Rule #4 — Obviously Dead

In cases where it is determined on arrival that a patient is "obviously dead", CTAS is required to be assigned and documented as a zero (0) on the E-PCR.

5

🕐 Transport Time Considerations

  • Short transport (<10 min): May not have opportunity to update CTAS level before arrival.
  • Long transport (≥20 min): Greater likelihood of condition change; more time to update ED of CTAS changes.
  • Always notify ED of changes to allow preparation of appropriate resources.
6

⚠ Hard-to-Assign Patients

If a patient seems difficult to assign an acuity level to because they don't fit any category, paramedics must either:

  • Discuss the case with SRCA medical control, or
  • Make a judgement based on their clinical experience.

Never leave a patient unassigned — always document a CTAS level.

Transport Destination Guidelines

1
Resuscitation

Nearest / closest most appropriate receiving facility capable of providing required care. No exceptions unless directed by medical control.

2
Emergent

Nearest / closest most appropriate receiving facility based on communication between paramedics, medical control, and receiving facility.

3 · 4 · 5
Urgent to Non-Urgent

Most available receiving facility. Greater flexibility in destination selection based on availability and patient preference.

CTAS Modifiers

▲ First Order Modifiers

First order modifiers are applied once the presenting complaint has been determined. They are divided into Group A (ABCs & Vital Signs) and Group B (Condition-Specific). Apply all that are relevant — do not stop at the first one found.

🩺

A & B — Respiratory Distress

  • CTAS 1 Absent/severely compromised respirations, SpO₂ <90%
  • CTAS 2 Severe distress, RR >30 or <10, SpO₂ 90–94%
  • CTAS 3 Moderate distress, RR 25–30, SpO₂ 94–97%
  • Assessed by: Respiratory rate, SpO₂, work of breathing, accessory muscle use, position
💓

C — Hemodynamic Stability

  • CTAS 1 Absent/severely compromised circulation, SBP <80, HR >140 or <40
  • CTAS 2 Compromised circulation, SBP 80–100, HR 120–140 or 40–50
  • CTAS 3 Mildly compromised, SBP 100–120, HR 100–120
  • Assessed by: HR, BP, skin color, capillary refill, diaphoresis
🧠

D — Level of Consciousness (GCS)

  • CTAS 1 GCS ≤8 (Unresponsive / P or U on AVPU)
  • CTAS 2 GCS 9–13 (Responds to Voice — V on AVPU)
  • CTAS 3 GCS 14 (Confused/disoriented, Alert but confused)
  • Use AVPU scale: Alert, Voice, Pain, Unresponsive
🔥

Pain Scale (0–10)

  • CTAS 2 Severe pain: 8–10/10
  • CTAS 3 Moderate pain: 4–7/10
  • Pain scale 4 (CTAS 3) is the most commonly applied modifier
  • Pain assessment must be consistent and documented on E-PCR
  • Use age-appropriate pain scales for pediatric patients
🩸

Bleeding Disorders

  • Applies to patients with hemophilia, on anticoagulants, or with liver failure
  • These patients are at higher risk for serious bleeding complications
  • Upgrade CTAS level by one level if bleeding disorder is present
  • Document the bleeding disorder on the E-PCR
🚗

Mechanism of Injury (MOI)

  • CTAS 2 High-energy MOI: MVC >60 km/h, ejection, rollover, death in same vehicle, pedestrian struck, fall >6m
  • CTAS 3 Moderate MOI: MVC <60 km/h, fall 3–6m, assault with weapon
  • MOI helps predict potential for occult injury even when vitals are normal

▼ Second Order Modifiers

Second order modifiers are more complaint-specific and are applied after first order modifiers. They cannot downgrade a CTAS level that was already set higher by the presenting complaint or first order modifiers.

🧸

Blood Glucose

  • CTAS 2 Blood glucose <60 mg/dl (hypoglycemia) with altered LOC
  • CTAS 2 Blood glucose >324 mg/dl (severe hyperglycemia)
  • Blood glucose <60 without altered LOC → CTAS 3
  • Always assess blood glucose in altered LOC patients
📈

Hypertension

  • CTAS 2 SBP >220 mmHg or DBP >130 mmHg
  • CTAS 3 SBP 180–220 mmHg or DBP 110–130 mmHg
  • Hypertensive crisis with end-organ symptoms → CTAS 2
  • Isolated hypertension without symptoms may be lower acuity
👶

Obstetrics (≥20 Weeks Gestation)

  • Applies to pregnant patients at 20 weeks gestation or more
  • Active labor, vaginal bleeding, or severe abdominal pain → CTAS 2
  • Decreased fetal movement, mild contractions → CTAS 3
  • Supine hypotension syndrome: position patient on left side
🧠

Mental Health Modifiers

  • CTAS 2 Actively suicidal or homicidal with plan and means
  • CTAS 2 Severe agitation, violent behavior, danger to self or others
  • CTAS 3 Suicidal ideation without plan, moderate agitation
  • Always assess safety of patient and crew

Pediatric CTAS

👶

Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)

Applied to all patients under 14 years of age

👤 Adult CTAS

  • Presenting complaint is primary driver
  • Vital signs based on adult normal ranges
  • Pain scale 0–10 (numeric or verbal)
  • No PAT assessment required
  • GCS used for LOC assessment
  • Obstetric modifier applies (≥20 wks)

👶 Pediatric CTAS (<14 years)

  • PAT performed first (before touching)
  • Age-specific vital sign norms required
  • Age-appropriate pain scales (FLACC, Faces)
  • Weight-based medication dosing
  • Pediatric-specific CEDIS categories
  • Fever thresholds differ by age

⚠️ PEDIATRIC RULE: Significant Tachycardia & Downgrade

When a child presents with significant tachycardia (CTAS 2 level), the paramedic must consider whether the tachycardia is due to distress/crying rather than a true pathological cause.

STEP 1 Allow the child to calm down (reduce stimulation, comfort measures).
STEP 2 Recheck vital signs once the child is calm.
IF IMPROVED Tachycardia resolved → Patient may be downgraded to an appropriate lower CTAS level.
IF PERSISTS Tachycardia still present → Classify as CTAS 2 (Emergent).

Pediatric-Specific Modifiers

🌡

Fever by Age

  • CTAS 2 Fever in infant <3 months (any temp >38°C)
  • CTAS 2 Fever >40°C in any child with toxic appearance
  • CTAS 3 Fever 38–40°C in child 3–36 months
  • Hypothermia (<36°C) in neonate → CTAS 1
💕

Pediatric Pain Assessment

  • Use FLACC scale for non-verbal/pre-verbal children
  • Use Faces Pain Scale for children 3–7 years
  • Use Numeric scale for children 8+ years
  • Parental report is valuable for baseline behavior
👶

Child Abuse / Neglect

  • Mechanism inconsistent with injury pattern → upgrade CTAS
  • Multiple injuries at different stages of healing
  • Delayed presentation for significant injuries
  • Document objectively; report per local protocol
💥

Pediatric Trauma MOI

  • CTAS 1 Traumatic arrest, penetrating head/torso injury
  • CTAS 2 Fall >3× child's height, MVC with significant damage
  • CTAS 3 Fall 1–3× child's height, bicycle collision
  • Children compensate well — deterioration can be sudden

🌎 CTAS Dynamicity — Real-Life Analogies

Just as CTAS level changes dynamically with every new clinical finding, many systems in daily life operate on the same principle — a single variable can change the entire outcome. These analogies sharpen understanding of CTAS dynamicity.

✈️

Airport Security Priority Lanes

A passenger enters the airport. Their base status is "standard traveller" (like a CTAS 4). But add one variable — a diplomatic passport — and they jump to priority lane (CTAS 2). Add another — medical emergency on board — and they reach the front of every queue (CTAS 1). Remove the emergency, and they return to priority (CTAS 2).

CTAS PARALLEL

Each new variable (passport type, emergency status, flight urgency) modifies the priority level — exactly as each CTAS modifier modifies the triage level. The highest-priority variable wins.

🚨

Emergency Vehicle Road Priority

A car on the road has a base priority level. Add a siren — priority increases. Add lights + siren + cardiac arrest patient on board — all traffic stops, every signal turns green. Remove the cardiac arrest patient (condition stabilised) — priority adjusts downward.

CTAS PARALLEL

The second CTAS level adjusts after intervention — just as the ambulance priority adjusts when the patient is stabilised. The first CTAS (arrival) is like the initial emergency call — it never changes retroactively.

🍳

The Chef's Recipe — Ingredient Variables

A dish starts as "mild" (CTAS 4). Add chilli — it becomes moderate (CTAS 3). Add ghost pepper — it becomes severe (CTAS 2). Add pure capsaicin extract — it becomes dangerous (CTAS 1). Each ingredient is a modifier. The final dish level is determined by the most extreme ingredient, not the average.

CTAS PARALLEL

In CTAS, the highest modifier wins — a CTAS 2 modifier overrides a CTAS 4 complaint. You never average the levels. One critical finding elevates the entire assessment — just as one extreme ingredient defines the dish.

🏠

Bank Loan Risk Assessment

A loan applicant has a good salary (base: low risk). But add previous bankruptcy — risk jumps to high. Add current debt default — risk becomes critical, loan denied. Each financial variable is a modifier. A single negative variable can override all positive ones.

CTAS PARALLEL

A patient with mild chest pain (CTAS 3) who is also on anticoagulants (bleeding disorder modifier) gets upgraded — one variable overrides the base assessment. A second order modifier cannot downgrade what a first order modifier has already elevated — just as a good salary cannot override a current default.

🌩

National Weather Alert System

Weather starts at Green (normal). Add strong winds — Yellow alert. Add heavy rain + flooding — Orange alert. Add tornado warning — Red alert (evacuate immediately). Each new meteorological variable escalates the alert. When the tornado passes, the alert de-escalates — but the record of the Red alert remains.

CTAS PARALLEL

The first CTAS level (arrival) is like the peak alert — it is permanently recorded and never retroactively changed, even if the patient improves. The second CTAS (departure) reflects the current state after intervention — like the post-tornado de-escalation.

🛡️

Military Threat Level — DEFCON System

DEFCON 5 = peacetime (CTAS 5). DEFCON 4 = increased intelligence watch (CTAS 4). DEFCON 3 = Air Force readiness (CTAS 3). DEFCON 2 = armed forces ready to deploy (CTAS 2). DEFCON 1 = nuclear war imminent — maximum readiness (CTAS 1). Each new intelligence variable changes the threat level.

CTAS PARALLEL

CTAS is a 5-level threat assessment system for the human body. Like DEFCON, each new clinical finding (modifier) can escalate the threat level. And like DEFCON, you never skip levels — you assess systematically through the 4-step process before assigning the final level.

The Universal Principle of Dynamic Priority

Whether it is an airport, an ambulance, a kitchen, a bank, the weather, or a battlefield — every critical system in the world uses the same logic as CTAS: collect all variables, apply the highest-priority finding, and assign the appropriate response level. CTAS is not a medical bureaucracy — it is the universal language of urgency, applied to the human body.

✅ When to Use CTAS — and When NOT to Use It

✅ WHEN to Apply CTAS

EVERY PREHOSPITAL PATIENT CONTACT

Apply CTAS at every direct patient contact — medical or trauma, adult or pediatric, regardless of apparent severity.

ON ARRIVAL AT SCENE (CTAS #1)

Immediately upon patient contact — before any interventions — to capture the true initial severity.

AT SCENE DEPARTURE (CTAS #2)

After assessment and initial treatment — to reflect the patient's current condition and guide transport decisions.

CONDITION CHANGE EN ROUTE (CTAS #3+)

Any time the patient's condition significantly changes during transport — document and communicate to receiving ED.

ALL AGE GROUPS

Adults and pediatric patients (<14 years) — using age-appropriate frameworks and vital sign norms.

❌ When NOT to Apply CTAS

MASS CASUALTY INCIDENTS (MCI)

When ≥3–5 patients overwhelm available resources, use START Triage instead. CTAS is a one-patient, detailed assessment — not a rapid mass-sorting tool.

DISPATCH / PRE-ARRIVAL PHASE

CTAS requires direct patient contact. Before arrival, dispatchers use MPDS/ProQA — not CTAS. CTAS begins only when the paramedic physically contacts the patient.

OBVIOUSLY DEAD PATIENTS (CTAS 0)

Document as CTAS 0 — but the full CTAS assessment process does not apply. Confirm death criteria and follow protocol.

AS A STANDALONE DIAGNOSTIC TOOL

CTAS assigns acuity, not diagnosis. It does not replace clinical assessment, treatment protocols, or medical direction. It is a triage classification, not a treatment guide.

RETROACTIVELY CHANGING CTAS #1

Never revise the first CTAS level after the fact — even if the patient improved dramatically. The initial severity is a permanent record.

🚫 CTAS Scope — Limitations & Boundaries

LIMITED TO

Prehospital patient contact by trained EMS personnel. Acuity classification only — not diagnosis, not treatment.

DOES NOT EXTEND TO

Mass casualty sorting, dispatch prioritisation, in-hospital triage, definitive diagnosis, or treatment decision-making.

DOES NOT REPLACE

MPDS/ProQA dispatch systems, START/JumpSTART MCI triage, clinical protocols, medical direction, or ED triage systems (CTAS ED version is separate).

CANNOT PREDICT

Final diagnosis, patient outcome, or in-hospital resource needs. It is a snapshot of acuity at a point in time — not a prognosis.

🎯 What in Medical Practice Goes Beyond CTAS?

🏥

In-Hospital Triage Systems

Emergency Department triage (ED-CTAS, Manchester Triage System, ESI) — separate systems that operate after hospital arrival. CTAS prehospital feeds into but does not replace ED triage.

🚑

Mass Casualty & Disaster Triage

START, JumpSTART, SALT, CESIRA — used when patient numbers exceed resources. CTAS is not designed for multi-casualty sorting.

🔬

Clinical Diagnosis & Differential

CTAS assigns acuity — it does not diagnose. Full clinical assessment, 12-lead ECG interpretation, stroke scales (FAST, NIHSS), trauma scoring (GCS, ISS) go beyond CTAS scope.

💊

Treatment Protocols & Medical Acts

CTAS guides urgency — not treatment. Medication administration, airway management, cardioversion, and controlled acts are governed by separate clinical protocols and medical direction.

📋

Quality Improvement & Outcome Tracking

The delta between CTAS #1 and CTAS #2 is a QI metric — but full outcome analysis, mortality review, and system performance evaluation require separate audit frameworks beyond CTAS documentation.

👥

Ethical, Legal & Social Dimensions

Informed consent, capacity assessment, advance directives, DNR orders, domestic violence identification, and social determinants of health are beyond CTAS — but must be considered in parallel.

🚫 What EMS & Paramedics Must NOT Be Deceived By During CTAS

😀

The "Looks Fine" Deception

A patient who appears calm and well-dressed may be CTAS 2. Appearance is not acuity. A silent MI, aortic dissection, or PE can present with a smiling, ambulatory patient. Modifiers reveal what the eyes miss.

📸

Normal Vital Signs = Normal Patient

Vital signs can be normal in early shock, compensated heart failure, and early sepsis. Never let normal vitals alone downgrade a patient — the presenting complaint and modifiers must be assessed independently.

👤

Patient Self-Reporting Bias

Patients minimise symptoms ("it's nothing"), exaggerate for attention, or cannot accurately describe their pain. Use objective modifiers — do not rely solely on patient self-report for CTAS assignment.

🧠

Chronic Condition Familiarity

"He always has chest pain" — this is a cognitive trap. Each call is a new assessment. A chronic patient can have an acute exacerbation. CTAS must be applied fresh every time, without anchoring to past calls.

🌞

Pediatric "Quiet Child" Fallacy

A quiet, non-crying child is NOT reassuring — it may indicate severe illness or decompensation. Children compensate until they crash. Use the PAT (Pediatric Assessment Triangle) — appearance, work of breathing, circulation to skin.

👑

Social / Demographic Bias

The CTAS protocol explicitly states: "Attitude and empathy are critical. Paramedics must remain consistent and non-judgmental. Prejudice can increase patient risk." Age, gender, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, or past behaviour must NEVER influence CTAS assignment.

📈

The "Treated = Improved" Trap

After administering oxygen or IV fluids, the patient may look better — but CTAS #1 must never be retroactively lowered. The initial severity is permanently documented. The second CTAS reflects current state after treatment.

📞

Dispatch Information Anchoring

The MPDS call may say "minor fall" — but on arrival the patient has a hip fracture and is in pain 9/10. CTAS begins fresh at patient contact. Never anchor to the dispatch priority when assigning CTAS.

🔎 Most Common Pitfalls in CTAS Practice

1

Stopping at the First Modifier Found

The CTAS protocol explicitly states: "Look for ALL modifiers that apply — do not stop at the first one found." Multiple modifiers may be present simultaneously, and the highest level wins.

2

Applying Second Order Modifier to Downgrade

A second order modifier must NOT downgrade the CTAS level when a higher level is already indicated by the presenting complaint or first order modifiers. It can only maintain or escalate.

3

Forgetting the Second CTAS Score

Both CTAS scores are mandatory. Documenting only CTAS #1 is an incomplete record. The second score is essential for QI, destination decisions, and ED handover communication.

4

Using Quick Look for CTAS 2–5

Quick Look can only assign CTAS Level 1. It cannot be used to place a patient in any other level. For CTAS 2–5, the full 4-step process (complaint → first order → second order modifiers) is mandatory.

5

Incorrect Pediatric Vital Sign Interpretation

Using adult vital sign norms for children leads to under-triage. A heart rate of 130 is normal for a toddler but alarming in an adult. Always use age-specific pediatric norms when assessing patients under 14.

6

Failing to Communicate CTAS to Receiving ED

The CTAS level must be communicated to the receiving institution — not just documented on the E-PCR. The ED needs this information to allocate resources before the patient arrives.

7

Ignoring Temperature as a Modifier

Temperature is listed as a first order modifier with the qualifier "if available." Many paramedics skip it. When a thermometer is available, temperature MUST be assessed — especially in pediatric patients and suspected sepsis.

8

Retroactively Changing CTAS #1

After a VSA patient is resuscitated, some paramedics change CTAS #1 to reflect the improved state. This is explicitly prohibited. Rule #3: VSA patients must remain CTAS 1 permanently, regardless of resuscitation outcome.

CLINICAL RULES GOVERNING BOTH MODIFIER TYPES
MUST — The Highest Level Always Governs
Regardless of whether the highest level comes from the presenting complaint, a First Order Modifier, or a Second Order Modifier — the highest level found anywhere in the assessment is the final CTAS level. No exceptions.
NEVER — Modifiers Cannot Downgrade
Neither First Order nor Second Order Modifiers can be used to lower a CTAS level below what the presenting complaint established. Modifiers are upgrade-only tools. A normal modifier finding does not lower the level — it simply does not upgrade it.
UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE — Abnormal = Upgrade
Any abnormal First Order Modifier finding must upgrade the CTAS level. Any positive Second Order condition must be applied. When in doubt about severity, assign the higher level. The patient's safety takes priority over administrative convenience.
DO NOT — Skip Second Order Because First Order Was High
Even if First Order Modifiers already place the patient at CTAS 1, Second Order Modifiers must still be assessed and documented. They provide critical clinical context for the receiving hospital and ensure comprehensive documentation in the e-PCR.
PRIORITY — Reassess Both Types Every 15 Minutes
Both First Order and Second Order Modifier findings must be formally reassessed every 15 minutes en route. Clinical status — physiological and contextual — can change rapidly. Each reassessment may result in a CTAS level upgrade.

■ CTAS 4: Minor bleeding · Easily controlled · No haemodynamic concern
MODIFIER MOIMechanism of Injury — High-Energy = Mandatory Upgrade
■ CTAS 1–2 High-Energy MOI:
High-speed MVC (>60 km/h) · Ejection from vehicle · Vehicle rollover · Pedestrian or cyclist struck by vehicle · Fall >6 metres · Penetrating injury to trunk, head, or neck · Blast/explosion · Crush injury · Death of another occupant in same vehicle
UNTIL PROVEN OTHERWISE
High-energy MOI must upgrade the CTAS level even if the patient appears stable. Occult injury is assumed until definitively ruled out at hospital.
DO NOT be reassured by normal vital signs alone in high-energy MOI. Compensation can mask serious injury.

STEP 6 — TRANSPORT DECISION · DESTINATION & PRIORITY
Step 6 · Transport
Transport Decision — Destination, Priority & Time
🚨 CTAS 1 & 2 — Critical Transport
MUST Transport to closest appropriate facility capable of definitive care.
MUST Pre-notify receiving hospital en route.
NEVER bypass a capable facility for patient preference.
Lights & sirens. Continuous monitoring. Reassess every 15 min.
If transport >15 min for CTAS 1 → consider air transport or ALS rendezvous.
⚠️ Transport Time Consideration
DO NOT allow transport time to alter the assigned CTAS level.
Transport time is a logistical factor, not a clinical one.
Long transport = more frequent reassessment, not a lower CTAS level.
Document transport time and all reassessments in e-PCR.
✅ CTAS 3–5 — Routine Transport
Appropriate facility — not necessarily closest.
Patient preference may be considered if clinically appropriate.
MUST Document CTAS level, all modifiers, and reassessments in e-PCR.
Reassess and upgrade immediately if condition deteriorates.

PEDIATRIC CTAS — SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS & ALGORITHM
PEDIATRIC CTAS — Modified Assessment Process
Step P1 — Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT)
PAT is the pediatric equivalent of Quick Look.
Abnormal PAT in any component = immediate clinical concern.
Abnormal PAT + any modifier = upgrade CTAS level immediately.
Pediatric Rules & Principles
MUST: Use age-specific vital sign norms (±1 SD normal, ±2 SD abnormal)
NEVER: Apply adult vital sign thresholds to children
PRIORITY: Floppy/limp child = CTAS 1 until proven otherwise
RULE OUT: Sepsis in any febrile infant <3 months — CTAS 2 minimum
MUST: Inconsolable crying = CTAS 2 minimum
NOTE: Children compensate remarkably well — deterioration is sudden and severe
MUST: Consider child abuse or neglect in all unexplained injuries
MUST: Involve parent/caregiver in history — 2–3 min interview concurrent with PAT
NOTE: Use FLACC or FACES pain scale for pre-verbal children
MUST: Assess developmental appropriateness of behaviour and interaction
Pediatric First Order Modifiers: Respiratory Rate · Heart Rate · SpO₂ · LOC (AVPU/GCS) · Temperature · Pain (FLACC/NRS) · Dehydration · Behaviour change
Pediatric Second Order Modifiers: Glucose · Fever pattern · Immunisation status · Neonatal age · Social concerns

💎 Pearls of Wisdom — Prehospital CTAS

Distilled clinical wisdom for the EMS professional in the field

💎

The Highest Level Always Governs

In CTAS, you never average. You never compromise. The single most critical finding — whether from the chief complaint, a first order modifier, or a second order modifier — determines the final level. One abnormal finding can elevate everything. When in doubt, go higher.

⚡

The First CTAS Is Sacred

The first CTAS score — recorded on arrival, before any intervention — is a permanent, unalterable record of initial severity. Even if the patient walks out of the ambulance smiling after your treatment, CTAS #1 stays. It is the truth of the moment you arrived.

👀

Look Beyond What You See

A calm patient in clean clothes can be in early septic shock. A quiet child may be decompensating. Normal vital signs do not mean a normal patient. CTAS was designed precisely for the cases that fool the eye. Trust the modifiers, not the appearance.

🔄

CTAS Is Dynamic, Not Static

A patient is not a fixed category — they are a moving target. Reassess every 15 minutes. A CTAS 3 can become a CTAS 1 in minutes. The paramedic who reassesses is the paramedic who saves lives. Never assume the first assessment is the last truth.

⚖️

Empathy Is a Clinical Tool

The CTAS framework explicitly states that attitude and empathy are critical to accurate triage. A paramedic who is non-judgmental, culturally sensitive, and emotionally present will elicit better history, detect subtle signs, and assign more accurate CTAS levels. Compassion improves clinical accuracy.

📞

When in Doubt — Call Medical Control

No paramedic is an island. The medical director exists to support your clinical decisions, not to judge them. Calling for guidance is not weakness — it is professional excellence. A single phone call can be the difference between a correct and a catastrophic decision.

👤

Every Patient Is a New Story

Familiarity is the enemy of accuracy. The patient you have seen 10 times before can present their 11th call as a CTAS 1. Anchor bias kills. Begin every assessment with fresh eyes, an open mind, and the full CTAS process — regardless of history.

📚

Document Everything — Always

The e-PCR is not bureaucracy — it is your clinical record, your legal protection, and the hospital's first window into your patient. Both CTAS scores are mandatory. Every modifier applied must be documented. If it isn't written, it didn't happen.

🌟

CTAS Is a Language — Speak It Fluently

When you say "CTAS 2" to the receiving ED, you are communicating an entire clinical picture — urgency, physiology, expected deterioration risk, and resource need — in a single number. Master CTAS and you master the language of prehospital urgency. Speak it with precision.

💕

Children Are Not Small Adults

Pediatric CTAS requires age-specific vital sign norms, the Pediatric Assessment Triangle, and age-appropriate pain scales. A heart rate of 130 is normal in an infant but alarming in an adult. Apply pediatric thresholds — never adult norms — to children under 14.

🎯

MOI Is a Modifier — Not a Diagnosis

A high-energy mechanism of injury mandates a minimum CTAS 2 — even if the patient is walking and talking. The body compensates. Internal injuries are invisible. "Until proven otherwise" is the rule — high-energy MOI equals high acuity until the hospital proves otherwise.

⚡

Speed and Accuracy Are Not Opposites

The 4-step CTAS process — Quick Look, Presenting Complaint, First Order Modifiers, Second Order Modifiers — is designed to be rapid and systematic simultaneously. With practice, it becomes second nature. A well-trained paramedic assigns CTAS accurately in under 60 seconds.
